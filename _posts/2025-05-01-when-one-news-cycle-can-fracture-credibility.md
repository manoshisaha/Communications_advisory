In Bangladesh's fast-moving media environment, organizations are increasingly vulnerable to rapid reputation damage. A single news cycle — one poorly handled press conference, one leaked document, one unanswered allegation — can fracture trust that took a decade to build.

## The Speed of Reputational Risk

The most dangerous assumption any institution can make is that reputational damage unfolds slowly enough to respond to. It does not. Digital platforms have compressed the timeline from incident to institutional crisis from days to hours.

## What Resilient Institutions Do Differently

The institutions that navigate these moments successfully share a common trait: they have a plan before the crisis arrives. They have mapped their stakeholders, identified their vulnerabilities, and built relationships with the media and regulatory environment that allow them to communicate credibly under pressure.

**Preparation is not paranoia.** It is the minimum standard of responsible leadership in today's Bangladesh.

## The Communication Imperative

Every organization in Bangladesh today should have:

1. A crisis communication protocol reviewed and rehearsed in the last 12 months
2. Clear internal decision-making authority for communications during a crisis
3. Pre-approved holding statements for common risk scenarios
4. Relationships with senior journalists and editors established before they are needed

The organizations that will define Bangladesh's next chapter are those that treat communication not as a department, but as a discipline.
